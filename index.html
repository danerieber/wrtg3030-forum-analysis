Forum Analysis

An examination of the different lenses through which public discourse and scientific research approach a contemporary issue

by Dane Rieber

Background

UK schools were closed due to COVID-19, so standardized tests like the GCSE1 could not be held according to Ofqual’s2 decision.

Ofqual used a method in which teachers gave estimated grades for each student for each subject. Teachers also provided rankings relative to other students with the same grade. These metrics were then processed by an algorithm that took into account each school’s historical performance and adjusted the grades accordingly.

Their goal was to make each school’s performance match with the results from previous years.

1 The General Certificate of Secondary Education
2 The Office of Qualifications and Examinations Regulation

Articles

Popular Article from the BBC

The BBC’s article briefly describes the situation and then presents data showing that a significant percentage of student’s grades were lower than their teachers’ estimates. They also outline how this system benefitted private schools, since their history of higher performance meant that their students’ grades were not lowered to the same degree as public schools. This evidently fails high-performing students at low-performing schools, whose grades would likely be more accurate within the context of all UK students rather than just one school.

The article wraps up by covering the government’s reversal of the decision. Finally, it presents a potential new problem of “grade inflation” due to the teacher-assigned grades flying above school averages. They predict that there may now be a strain on universities with the influx of students who can qualify for their programs.

Scholarly Article from the OSF

The scholarly article focused its attention on surveying affected students to gather their opinions on the fairness of exam cancellations and calculated grades. After providing background information, the paper explores student sentiments, taking into account patterns with demographics as well. Their findings showed that students were concerned for their future and their mental health, worrying about the potential impacts of being the “COVID-19 generation” -- specifically what their unique grades will mean for their chances of college acceptance and employment.

It seemed that many students attributed the unfairness to structural inequalities in the UK’s education system, expressing concern over racism and classism playing a significant role. Since the system relied on the opinion of teachers, students worried about personal biases finding their way into the results.

Forums

BBC

The BBC’s self-description encapsulates what people ultimately expect from a large, responsible news outlet. While “impartial and independent” may be nearly impossible to enforce uniformly throughout their organization, their inclusion of their purpose to “entertain” may serve as a testament to their transparency -- it is not in their interest to hide such motives from the reader. Even though entertainment can sometimes extend to exaggeration of the truth, the BBC nevertheless acknowledges entertainment’s importance in the media landscape today.

Arguably, the juxtaposition of entertainment and education in their description may indicate an implied mission: discovering the optimal balance between the two. Neglecting to include this detail could suggest either an education-only focus (uncommon in mainstream media) or a dishonest attempt from the outlet to ignore the realities of their reporting. All implications and intentions aside, the BBC’s synopsis is brief and apt.

OSF

The Open Science Framework is a product of COS (Center for Open Science). COS is very vague with their descriptions which are, in reality, more of mission statements instead. The goals outlined in the statements speak to the nature of the content that one would expect to find on their platform: open, collaborative, yet diligent research.

It seems that openness and integrity may be counterintuitive, specifically for the scientific community. The means of discourse and publication within science are typically reserved for those that prove themselves qualified enough to engage in its language and methodology. This usually has the side effect that only properly conducted studies make their way into the spotlight. It can be argued, though, that COS’s emphasis on collaboration -- treating each project not as owned by the author but as owned by the community -- can remediate the faults of open publication, since the reliability of the research is everyone’s responsibility. In addition, opening scientific publication to the public should not decrease the competitiveness of top scientists who are attuned to the scholarly discourse, and it should rather draw attention to the quality of their work in reference to other, less disciplined public work.

Whether or not OSF’s openness actually fosters integrity, perhaps papers from their website should be approached with diligence and skepticism regardless.

Comparison

To compare the two forums, consider publishing a work originally from one forum to the other forum -- how successful would it be? Would a reader of the BBC care about, or even understand, a painfully thorough rundown of every finding leading to the scientific conclusions they’re interested in? Would a reader of OSF papers want more information to substantiate the claims made in news articles?

A news article published to COS would likely be skipped by the majority of readers, as news articles are typically just a composite of existing facts or findings that fail to provide new information. The only exception to this is current events in which the news provides the information. In research, current events are typically studied in reference to a collection of similar events rather than just the most recent event itself, so these articles would still likely be irrelevant on OSF. Scientific publications do not exist to grab your attention, so entertainment and public reception is not of concern to readers or writers.

Likewise, a study on OSF wouldn’t see much love on the BBC. Most people probably find the biggest barrier for reading such a paper is the time commitment. In fact, this very analysis is much longer than the BBC’s article itself, because this is not a summary of existing analysis, but the actual analysis itself. Running through reasoning takes more effort than people are willing to tackle.

That is not to say that lack of reasoning is a bad thing. If you were to grab an atlas of your city, find the nearest McDonald’s, chart all major roads in its proximity, obtain data on traffic conditions and speed limits, gather information about the weather forecast, and finally conduct a case study on the timing of the traffic lights and the methods needed to avoid stops, then you could undoubtedly find the fastest possible method in which to reach the nearest McDonald’s. Or, you could trust that Google Maps has done all of that already and even done it well enough to get you there very quickly, and you can happily enjoy your FDA-approved Big Mac -- that is unless you would rather study its safety for human consumption yourself.

Google Maps and the FDA not only prove themselves trustworthy as authorities, but also prove themselves as authorities due to their record of trustworthiness. It is with an almost insane (yet commendable) distrust of authority that a reader of the BBC would require a totality of reasoning and evidence rather than trust the source as an authority on accurate information reporting. This is why consumers of mainstream media don’t care about science unless they want to, and if they want to, they are keen to get more information elsewhere (such as Neil deGrass Tyson’s Twitter1).

A Closer Look

at the articles' introductions
BBC

The BBC’s intro is brief and straightforward, immediately establishing their topic as Ofqual’s reversal of their decision to algorithmically adjust student’s grades. The BBC also notes that this followed from an “uproar,” grabbing readers’ attention to a conflict that may be of interest to them (or already has been of interest to them). Two sentences is all that the BBC needs for their hook -- a dramatic event of national importance that involves children’s well-being and the mysterious inner-workings of new technology. It’s likely that they kept the intro brief under the reasonable assumption that many readers would be invested in the situation already.

OSF

The scholarly article’s introduction first covers basic background information and the extenuating circumstances leading to Ofqual’s controversial decision. Then, identifying its three main topics in a numbered list, the paper is said to explore the impact and fairness of the grading algorithm along with an evaluation of the support systems available for affected students. The introduction also emphasizes the gravity of the situation, stating the importance of A-level grades and their weight over the direction of a young student’s life.

Comparison

The most glaring difference between the two articles’ introductions is how they introduce the grading algorithm. The BBC sees no need for background information behind the decision, instead introducing it as a failed plan tainted by public outcry. The scholarly article, however, identifies the unprecedented challenges that arose from COVID-19 as a precursor to multiple tough decisions that administrators encountered much difficulty trying to solve. The scholarly paper seems to regard the algorithm more as an attempt rather than as a failure.

Audience

BBC

The BBC is a massive news outlet that reaches an audience consisting of all types of people with varying areas of expertise. As with any popular news outlet, their articles are written and published with the intent to be highly accessible, therefore requiring no significant expertise from the reader and no significant investment of the reader’s time. In addition, it is in the BBC’s best interest to captivate readers and increase their chances of establishing themselves as the reader’s primary news outlet.

In short, their articles are brief, simple, and likely to be entertaining. It’s important to note that this was published as an “explainer” article, a special type of article that answers common questions about current events.

OSF

This paper was published through OSF (Open Science Framework), a product of COS (Center for Open Science). COS intends to streamline research publication and increase the accessibility to scientific work for both readers and writers.

Often in academia, scientific journals require an institutional account or even payment to access them -- and for writers, they require connections with publishers or professionals. By publishing this article using OSF, the author can avoid such barriers and increase access to their work. This is not to say, however, that the article is intended for the general public, as OSF clearly establishes itself as a product for the scientific community.

Comparison

While both publishers emphasize openness and accessibility, it’s important to make the distinction between the general public and the scientific community. The scholarly paper is still decorated appropriately with the sophisticated language from the halls of academia, but it also takes care not to restrict its content to the niche understanding of field experts. To provide some comparison, the scholarly article is readable by all scholars rather than only statisticians or administrators in education, and the news article is readable by all people rather than only academics. Nevertheless, the difference in the articles’ target audience bleeds into their writing -- obvious even by taking a mere glance at the structure of the two articles.

Structure

BBC

As with most news articles, the length of the content decreases to about half by removing advertisements, pictures, and graphs. However, uncharacteristic to most news, the BBC’s article is fairly information-rich. Bulleted lists, statistics, definitions, graphs, and analysis all find their way into the article. This is most likely a consequence of being written as a BBC explainer article, of whose focus lies more on providing frequently requested information.

OSF

Nearly a ten-page report, rich with statistics and information, this read is not really suited for a bathroom break at work (especially if you work at Amazon). This paper is thorough and informative, aiming to draw conclusions from relevant empirical evidence and sufficient background information. Because of this, the paper is quite lengthy and dense, and may need multiple passes for its ideas to become fully saturated within the reader’s mind.

Overall, it follows the expected structure of a scientific paper, divided into the expected sections and littered with citations. One interesting thing to note is the absence of graphs. Perhaps the author saw it unnecessary, as most of their statistics were simply compiled into bulleted lists.

Comparison

The scientific paper’s absence of graphs may be concerning for some. In fact, there are places where bar graphs could quite easily be created and would make the statistics more presentable. While this may seem uncharacteristic of a scholarly paper, this particular paper actually puts an emphasis on its qualitative data, specifically the interviews with affected students. This seems odd: the BBC focuses more on numbers than scholars studying the situation. Perhaps the scholars wanted to take a new approach that doesn’t rely on numerical evidence or mathematics -- which would be quite appropriate considering that numbers had just failed UK students with their newly calculated grades.

The BBC article set out to prove that the system malfunctioned, producing the wrong result and therefore resulting in a systematic unfairness. The scholarly article set out to prove something even stronger -- that is, that the lives of the students themselves were not actually improved by the algorithm (at least from the perspective of the students).
